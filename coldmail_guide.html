The Truth About Cold Emails

Most students do it completely wrong.

Either 500 copy-pasted generic emails hoping something sticks, or 3 hours on one perfect email to one company. Both fail. The actual winning formula is simple.

High-Quality Personalisation + Systematic Volume

Decision makers receive 30–50 cold emails a week. 95% start with "I am writing to express my interest in…" — one email that references their actual work stands out immediately. You're not just selling yourself. You're showing you understand what they do and why it matters.

Why active LinkedIn posters respond more
More raw materialThey've given you real content to write something worth responding to
They value engagementPeople who post publicly appreciate thoughtful responses to their work
Easier to personaliseTheir posts reveal communication style, values, and what impresses them

02
Step Two

Research Like a Strategist

Go deep on each person before writing a single word. Find 2–3 genuine points of connection — something real, not flattery.

What to research
Their LinkedIn postsLast 5–10 posts: What do they talk about? What language do they use? What do they care about?
Their LinkedIn profileCareer path, current team, how they describe their work. Copy the full page into Claude/GPT.
Company recent newsFunding rounds, launches, expansions, awards — real context for why you're reaching out now
Their team's workWhat does the department they lead actually do? What are they probably hiring for?
Shared groundSame college, city, industry event, mutual connection, content you actually liked
The golden rule

Talk more about them than you. Once you've shown you genuinely understand what they do and why it matters, then — and only then — show how your experience overlaps with their work.

Step Three

Use AI to Research Faster

You don't need to manually read everything. Use Claude or ChatGPT to extract insights from their LinkedIn profile in 2 minutes.

How to use it
Step 1Go to their LinkedIn profile
Step 2Press Ctrl+A (select all) → Ctrl+C (copy)
Step 3Paste into Claude or ChatGPT with the prompt below
Step 4Also paste their last 5–10 LinkedIn posts
The LinkedIn Research Prompt
Analyse this LinkedIn profile. Tell me:
1. What does this person care about most professionally?
2. What is their communication style (formal / casual / technical / conversational)?
3. What recurring themes or values appear in their posts?
4. What would genuinely impress them in an email from a student?
5. What one specific aspect of their work should I reference to signal I've done my homework?

[Paste full LinkedIn profile + recent posts below this line]

The more posts you paste in, the better the analysis. Prioritise people who post 2–3x a week — they give AI enough to build a clear picture of what they value and how they communicate.

04
Step Four

Write the Email

The structure is the same whether you're hunting for an internship, a job, or a brand partnership. What changes is the content — because you've done the research.

The 4-Part Framework
One structure. Every outreach. Personalise the content, not the bones.
Part 1
The Hook
Reference something specific: their post, recent company news, their exact role title, a genuine personal connection. Not "I'm very interested in your esteemed organisation."
Part 2
The Connection
Talk about their team's work, the problem they solve, why what they do matters to you. Make it specific to them, not generic to the industry.
Part 3
The Congruence
Now bring in your experience. One relevant project, one result, one story. Connect it explicitly to what they do. Not a CV dump.
Part 4
The Ask
One clear, low-friction ask. A 15–20 min call. A quick reply. Not "Please consider me for all available opportunities." Simple. Specific.

The Follow-Up Sequence

Most deals happen on the second or third touch.

Most students send one email and give up. Here is the exact sequence.

Day 1
First email
Full personalised email. Subject line tested. Attachment if relevant.
Day 5–7
Follow-up 1
One line. Nothing more.
"Hi [Name] — wanted to bump this up in case it got buried. Happy to share more context if helpful."
Day 12–14
Follow-up 2 — add something new
A result you just got. A piece of content you made. Show momentum, not desperation.
"Hi [Name] — sharing an update since my last mail: [one line of news]. Would still love to connect."
Day 20+
Move on
If still no response — move on. Don't send a fourth email. Mark the contact for revisiting in 3 months with new context.

The golden rule of follow-ups: Every follow-up should either add new value or ask a simpler question. Never send a follow-up that is just "Did you see my email?" — that is the fastest way to get blocked.

Pre-Send Checklist

Run every email through this. No exceptions.

1 — Subject Line
Is it specific enough that only this person would fully get it?
Could it have been sent to any other company unchanged? If yes — rewrite it.
2 — The Hook
Does the first line reference something specific about them (not the company, them)?
Would they know you actually read their profile / post / news?
3 — About Them
Did you spend more words on them than on yourself?
Is there at least one specific thing you referenced that shows genuine research?
4 — Your Story
Does your experience section connect explicitly to what they do?
Did you lead with one strong result — not a list of everything you've done?
5 — The Ask
Is there exactly one ask?
Is it low-friction? (A call, a reply — not "please consider me for all openings")
6 — The Tone
Does it sound like a human wrote it — not a LinkedIn post generator?
Is it appropriately casual or formal for who you're emailing?
7 — The Basics
Name spelled correctly?
No generic filler phrases ("esteemed organisation", "I would be honoured")?
Signature includes phone + LinkedIn?
Common Mistakes

Stop making these.

Opening with "I"
Start with them. Not with yourself. "I am writing to…" is the most common and most boring opener.
Listing your CV
One experience, one result. Not a paragraph of everything you've ever done.
Generic compliments
"Your company is doing amazing work in this space." Means nothing. Be specific or say nothing.
Long emails
Under 200 words for cold outreach. They don't know you yet. Earn the long read first.
Formal tone everywhere
Match their tone. A startup founder who posts memes doesn't want "Dear Sir/Madam, I am writing to…"
No research on the person
Researching the company is table stakes. Researching the person is what gets you replies.
One email and give up
Follow up. Twice. With something new each time. Most replies come after the first follow-up.
Forgetting the ask
Every email must end with one specific, low-friction ask. Not a vague "hope to hear from you."
The one question to ask before hitting send

"If I were this person, and I received this email — would I actually reply?"

If the honest answer is no — rewrite the opening.
If it's maybe — rewrite the subject line.
If it's yes — send it.
